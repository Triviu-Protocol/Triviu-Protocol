Lesson 02 · The fee wall

Before profit, there is a wall. It is made of fees.

A cycle does not start at zero. Each swap takes a cut, and three swaps compound into a wall the mispricing has to clear before a single unit of gas is paid. This is the number that quietly decides most cycles — so we compute it in the open.

AI-GENERATED   Written to be checked, not trusted. The wall below is 1 / 0.997³ ≈ 1.009 — arithmetic from whitepaper §3, watchable in the simulator. Possibility is not probability.

The fee compounds: 0.997 to the N

From Lesson 01: a 0.30% pool keeps 99.7% of your effective input, so γ = 0.997. A triangular cycle is three swaps in a row, so the fee applies three times — it compounds.

fee survival over N hops = γN = 0.997N

For three hops, 0.997³ = 0.99103. You keep about 99.1% and lose roughly 0.9% to fees alone — before slippage, before gas, before anyone competes with you. Turned around, the raw mispricing you are chasing has to be at least that big just to break even on fees:

γ³ · PAB · PBC · PCA > 1   ⟹   product of the three rates > 1 / 0.997³ ≈ 1.009

That 0.9% is the first honest wall. Most transient discrepancies across liquid pools are smaller than it — which is exactly why they never make it into a profitable transaction.

More hops is a taller wall, not more chances

It is tempting to think that adding hops adds opportunities. It adds fees. Each extra swap multiplies survival by another 0.997, so the wall you must clear grows with every leg:

A five-hop cycle has to find a mispricing over 1.5% before it does anything but lose money. Longer paths look like more ways to win; the arithmetic makes them harder.

Then slippage and gas, on top

The wall is only the fees. On top of it sit two more costs. Slippage — from Lesson 01, your own trade moves each pool's price against you, worse the more you send, which is why an edge cannot simply be scaled up. And gas — a flat cost in the chain's native token that the profit has to cover regardless of size. The cycle runs on-chain only if the output clears principal plus a minProfit set above gas; otherwise the whole transaction reverts and you lose only the gas.

execute only if   Δout ≥ Δ0 + minProfit   (minProfit ≥ gas)

Stack the three together — the ~0.9% fee wall, slippage that caps your size, and a gas floor — and you have, before competition is even mentioned, why the expected result for an individual tends toward zero. Add professional searchers and sub-second auctions (Lesson 04) and the picture is complete. None of this is discouragement; it is the filter the math forces, published so you can run it yourself.

Lesson 03 · Anatomy of a real cycle

Three swaps, one transaction, one condition.

A cycle is not three trades you hope work out. It is a single atomic transaction that either clears one arithmetic condition and pays you, or reverts and leaves nothing exposed. Once you see that condition, the whole design falls into place — including why it can never take your funds.

All three legs, or nothing

The executor takes the route as input, gets the capital from you (or a flash loan), and runs the three swaps A → B → C → A. At the end, one check decides everything:

finalBalance = balanceOf(executor)
if (finalBalance < principal + minProfit) revert UnprofitableCycle()

If the cycle came back with at least your principal plus a set minProfit, it succeeds and returns principal plus the net result to you. If it came back short — for any reason, on any leg — the whole transaction reverts. No half-finished position is left sitting on the chain; you lose only the gas. That is what "atomic" buys you: the market risk of being caught mid-trade is gone. The cost risk is not.

Nothing to drain

The executor is stateless: it holds no balances between transactions and has no deposit function. Your capital enters and leaves inside the same transaction, signed by your own key. There is no pot of user funds anywhere in the system for anyone — including the protocol — to take. Non-custody here is not a policy; it is the shape of the contract.

Optimal size, not maximal size

From Lesson 01: your own trade moves each pool's price against you, and worse the more you send. So profit as a function of trade size is a hill, not a ramp — trade too little and you leave money on the table; trade too much and your own slippage erases the edge. There is a single finite best size.

The size decision
Too smallclears the fee wall but captures little of the gap
Best size Δ₀*the finite peak — the engine computes it in closed form
Too largeyour slippage closes the very gap you were chasing

The engine evaluates the profit at that best size, subtracts gas and the success fee, and only submits if what remains is positive. And minProfit is set at or above gas — so a cycle that would merely break even never runs. The default is dry_run: it computes and simulates, and sends nothing, until you deliberately say otherwise.

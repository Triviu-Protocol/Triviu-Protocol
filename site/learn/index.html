AMMs from zero

A constant-product pool holds two tokens under x · y = k. The price is only the ratio of the reserves, so every trade moves it. Arbitrage exists because the same pair can price differently across pools — a triangular cycle closes that gap, or it reverts.

02

The fee wall and the math

Each swap charges a fee on the input — 0.30% on QuickSwap/SushiSwap, 0.25% on PancakeSwap. Over N hops it compounds to 0.997^N: a three-hop cycle loses about 0.9% before it starts. The price product must beat that wall plus slippage plus gas, or it reverts. More hops means a taller wall, not more chances.

Anatomy of a real cycle

Three swaps run inside one atomic transaction: either it clears the minProfit condition and returns your principal plus profit, or the whole thing reverts and you lose only gas. Volume, pool depth and gas decide it — and oversizing a good edge kills it through slippage.

04

MEV and the searchers

Wherever atomic backrunning is profitable, a small professional population captures it in the same block through priority-fee auctions. A study of Polygon found a searcher pool that rarely exceeded ~15 entities, typically 5–8 active, taking the overwhelming majority. For an individual, after gas and competition, the expected result tends to zero or negative.

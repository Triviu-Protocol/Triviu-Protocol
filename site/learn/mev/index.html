Lesson 04 · MEV & the searchers

The seat at this table is already taken.

The fee wall explains why most cycles don't clear. This lesson explains who takes the ones that do. Atomic arbitrage is not an open field — it is a closed, professional game played in sub-second auctions, and the public data on exactly how closed is the honest number most projects hide.

A closed professional game

Wherever atomic backrunning is profitable, a small professional population captures it in the same block, through priority-fee auctions. An independent study of the FastLane/Atlas auction flow on Polygon — 223,356 opportunity transactions over ten months — found how few players that really is:

~15
most searchers active in any single week
17
unique searchers across the whole 10-month period
~250ms
the sealed-bid auction window per opportunity

Typically just 5–8 are active at a time. They compete inside that quarter-second window to detect the discrepancy, compute the route, and submit a bid with an execution bundle — and the study documents a winner's-curse dynamic where experienced players progressively raise their bids. A well-tuned agent wins a large share precisely because of strategy and infrastructure, not open access.

Where the value actually goes

A separate census of Polygon atomic-arbitrage MEV — about 23 million blocks — estimated roughly US$12M extracted, around 90% of all backrunning MEV on the chain. And of that value, more than 75% flows to validators (through bids and gas), with the searchers keeping the rest. Read the two facts together: a handful of professionals compete in sub-second auctions for a pool most of which is captured by the block producers above them.

What this means for you, plainly

That is the market an individual with ordinary hardware is invited — by the usual "profit bot" — to enter. After gas and fee, the expected result for most individuals tends toward zero or negative. And this is chain-general: adding Arbitrum or BSC changes the venue, not the economics. Triviu publishes this because it is the difference between a tool and a trap — the proposition was never "you will profit," it is "you will be able to verify," including verifying, from public data, that the profit is unlikely.

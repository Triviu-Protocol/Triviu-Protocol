Lesson 01 · AMMs from zero

The price is a ratio, and every trade moves it.

Before you can read an arbitrage, you have to read a pool. There is no order book and no matching engine here — just two piles of tokens and one rule that sets the price. Once you see that rule, price impact and the fee stop being jargon.

The one rule: x · y = k

A constant-product pool holds a reserve of two tokens, x and y. It commits to a single invariant: the product of the reserves stays constant across any swap.

x · y = k

Nobody quotes a price. The price is implied by the reserves: the price of one token is simply the other reserve divided by its own. That is the whole pricing engine — a division, read in whichever direction you are pricing.

price of a token = ( the other reserve ) / ( its own reserve )

Why every trade moves the price

To keep k constant, taking tokens out of one side forces tokens into the other. The ratio changes, so the price changes — on the very trade you just made. This is price impact, and it is not a fee or a bug: it is the invariant doing its job. The output for an input Δx is exact and on-chain:

Δy = ( γ · Δx · y ) / ( x + γ · Δx )

The γ is where the fee lives. Rather than tax the output, the pool shrinks the effective input: for a 0.30% pool, γ = 0.997, so only 99.7% of what you send actually pushes against the reserves. The withheld 0.3% stays in the pool — that is how liquidity providers earn. On PancakeSwap the fee is 0.25%, so γ = 0.9975; the engine reads the rate per pool instead of assuming one.

Worked example — one swap
Pool2,000 USDC · 1 WETH  (k = 2,000). Spot price P = 2,000 USDC per WETH.
You send200 USDC (10% of the USDC reserve)
Effective200 × 0.997 = 199.4 (the 0.3% fee stays in the pool)
You get199.4 × 1 / (2,000 + 199.4) = 0.0907 WETH
You paid200 / 0.0907 ≈ 2,206 USDC per WETH, not 2,000 — the gap is slippage
After≈ 2,200 USDC · 0.9093 WETH → new spot ≈ 2,419. Your own trade moved it.

Send a smaller order and the paid price sits closer to 2,000; send a larger one and it runs away faster. To first order, a trade worth 1% of the reserve costs about 1% in price — and the cost grows faster than linearly as you scale. Keep that fact: it is why an edge cannot simply be sized up.

Where arbitrage comes from

The same pair can sit at different ratios in different pools — one is momentarily cheap, another momentarily dear. A triangular cycle A → B → C → A is an attempt to walk that gap and come back holding more A than you started with. Either the numbers close and the trade returns your principal plus profit, or it reverts and you are out only gas.
